Carbon math

These circles representat the amount of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere that is unsafe for life, the amount that is added every year, and the IPCC estimate of annual carbon removal required by 2050 to keep global warming under 2°C in a moderate or high emissions scenario like the current one.

Current atmospheric carbon dioxide debt is about 945 Gt.

Put another way, there are currently at least 945 billion tons more of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere than is safe for life on Earth. This is current ppm minus safe ppm, assuming 350 ppm is safe.

Sources: James Hansen, NASA.

~ 945 Gt

Global annual emissions from human activity were about 36 Gt in 2019 and are roughly the same or greater today.

This does not include biosphere emissions, such as wildfire and permafrost thaw.

Source: Our World in Data.

~ 36 Gt

The IPCC estimate of annual carbon removal required by 2050 to keep global warming under 2°C in a moderate or high emissions scenario like the current one is 10 Gt.

10 Gt

Annual removal on this scale is historically unprecedented. If we accomplish it, and dramatically slash emissions, we can expet to limit global warming to under 2°C by 2100, which is widely accepted to be the uppermost limit of warming our planet can tolerate without becoming dramatically more unstable.

Source: IPCC.

Why, what, how

I made this because I was confused about how ppm, annual emissions, and carbon removal goals are related. I wanted to get a rough sense of how much more CO2 is in the atmosphere than should be there, the rate at which we're adding more, and the size of the goal for removing it — and the relative scale between all three.

The math for the circles is based on the sources below and a back-of-envelop formula for converting gigatonnes (Gt) of CO2 emissions into the atmosphere to ppm: 1 ppm ≈ 15Gt CO2 emissions (7.5Gt CO2 remains in the air; 7.5Gt CO2 absorbed by the ocean and biosphere). The formula is described by Klaus Lackner (on slide 7). I would welcome advice and corrections and additional sources.

The circles are simple pixel math. After the ppm and CO2 calculations, I took the square root of the results to calculate a diameter for the circles and used CSS to represent them in relative terms to each other, with pixels as the unit.
